Hangul (한글) Alphabet

Hangul is the official writing system of the Korean language, and it's considered one of the most scientific alphabets in the world. It consists of 14 consonants and 10 vowels. Mastering Hangul is the first step to reading and writing Korean!

Vowels (모음)

These are the ten basic vowels of Hangul. They form the core of every syllable.

아 (a) 아버지 (abeoji)
야 (ya) 야구 (yagu)
어 (eo) 어머니 (eomeoni)
여 (yeo) 여자 (yeoja)
오 (o) 오이 (oi)
요 (yo) 요리사 (yorisa)
우 (u) 우유 (uyu)
유 (yu) 유리 (yuri)
으 (eu) 이름 (ireum)
이 (i) 의사 (uisa)

Consonants (자음)

These are the fourteen basic consonants. They combine with vowels to form syllables.

ㄱ (g/k) 가방 (gabang)
ㄴ (n) 나라 (nara)
ㄷ (d/t) 다리 (dari)
ㄹ (r/l) 라면 (ramyeon)
ㅁ (m) 마음 (ma-eum)
ㅂ (b/p) 바지 (baji)
ㅅ (s) 사과 (sagwa)
ㅇ (silent/ng) 안녕 (annyeong)
ㅈ (j) 자다 (jada)
ㅊ (ch) 차 (cha)
ㅋ (k) 코 (ko)
ㅌ (t) 타다 (tada)
ㅍ (p) 피아노 (piano)
ㅎ (h) 하다 (hada)

Pro Tip:

Consonant 'ㅇ' is silent when it's at the beginning of a syllable, but it makes an 'ng' sound when at the end. For example: '안' (an) and '강' (gang).

Essential Korean Phrases

Learn these must-know phrases to start a conversation and navigate daily situations in Korea. Practice saying them out loud!

안녕하세요
[annyeonghaseyo]
Hello / Hi / Good morning/afternoon
This is the most common polite greeting used in almost any situation.
감사합니다
[gamsahamnida]
Thank you
A formal and polite way to express gratitude.
죄송합니다
[joesonghamnida]
I'm sorry
Used to apologize formally and sincerely.
네 / 아니요
[ne] / [aniyo]
Yes / No
Fundamental responses to questions.
괜찮아요
[gwaenchanayo]
It's okay / I'm fine
A versatile phrase for responding that you are okay or that something is fine.
이게 뭐예요?
[ige mwoyeyo?]
What is this?
A useful phrase for pointing at something you don't recognize.
화장실이 어디예요?
[hwajangsiri eodiyeyo?]
Where is the restroom?
A practical phrase for finding a bathroom.
반갑습니다
[bangapseumnida]
Nice to meet you
A polite and respectful greeting when meeting someone for the first time.

Pro Tip:

Many Korean greetings and phrases have different levels of formality. The phrases above are in the polite form (-요), which is a safe and respectful choice for most situations. Using informal language with someone older or of higher social standing is often considered rude.
