Korean Sentence Structure

Korean follows a Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) word order, which is different from English (SVO). Understanding this fundamental difference is key to forming correct Korean sentences.

English (SVO)

I eat rice

Korean (SOV)

저는 밥을 먹어요
jeoneun babeul meogeoyo

More Examples

저는 K-pop을 좋아해요
jeoneun K-pop-eul johahaeyo
I like K-pop
저는 (I) + K-pop을 (K-pop) + 좋아해요 (like)
친구가 책을 읽어요
chinguga chaegeul ilgeoyo
My friend reads a book
친구가 (friend) + 책을 (book) + 읽어요 (reads)

Korean Particles (조사)

Particles are small words that attach to nouns to indicate their grammatical role in a sentence. They're crucial for understanding Korean!

은/는
Topic Particle
Marks the topic of the sentence
저는 학생이에요 I am a student
Use 는 after vowels, 은 after consonants
이/가
Subject Particle
Marks the subject performing the action
비가 와요 Rain is falling
Use 가 after vowels, 이 after consonants
을/를
Object Particle
Marks the direct object
음악을 들어요 I listen to music
Use 를 after vowels, 을 after consonants
에
Location/Time Particle
Indicates location or time
학교에 가요 I go to school
3시에 만나요 Let's meet at 3 o'clock
에서
Location Particle
Indicates where an action takes place
카페에서 공부해요 I study at a cafe
와/과, 하고
And/With Particle
Connects nouns or indicates "with"
친구와 영화 봐요 I watch a movie with a friend
와 after vowels, 과 after consonants

Verb Conjugations (동사 활용)

Korean verbs change their endings based on formality level, tense, and other factors. Here are the most common conjugation patterns:

Present Tense (현재형)

Polite Form (-요)

가다 (to go) → 가요 (go/goes)
먹다 (to eat) → 먹어요 (eat/eats)
하다 (to do) → 해요 (do/does)

Formal Form (-습니다)

가다 (to go) → 갑니다 (go/goes)
먹다 (to eat) → 먹습니다 (eat/eats)
하다 (to do) → 합니다 (do/does)

Past Tense (과거형)

Polite Past (-었/았어요)

가다 (to go) → 갔어요 (went)
먹다 (to eat) → 먹었어요 (ate)
보다 (to see) → 봤어요 (saw)

Formal Past (-었/았습니다)

가다 (to go) → 갔습니다 (went)
먹다 (to eat) → 먹었습니다 (ate)
보다 (to see) → 봤습니다 (saw)

Future Tense (미래형)

Will/Going to (-(으)ㄹ 거예요)

가다 (to go) → 갈 거예요 (will go)
먹다 (to eat) → 먹을 거예요 (will eat)
하다 (to do) → 할 거예요 (will do)

Intention (-(으)ㄹ게요)

가다 (to go) → 갈게요 (I'll go)
하다 (to do) → 할게요 (I'll do)

Honorific Language (존댓말)

Korean has different levels of formality and respect. Understanding when and how to use honorific language is crucial for proper communication.

Formal/Polite (존댓말)

Use with strangers, older people, or in formal situations
안녕하세요 Hello
감사합니다 Thank you
죄송합니다 I'm sorry

Casual (반말)

Use with close friends or people younger than you
안녕 Hi/Bye
고마워 Thanks
미안 Sorry

Subject Honorific (주체 존대)

When the subject is someone you respect
선생님이 오세요 The teacher is coming
할머니께서 주무세요 Grandmother is sleeping

🎵 K-pop Connection

In K-pop, you'll often hear different speech levels:

  • Formal speech in interviews and award ceremonies
  • Polite speech when talking to fans
  • Casual speech between group members in behind-the-scenes content

Forming Questions (의문문)

Korean questions can be formed in several ways. Here are the most common patterns:

Yes/No Questions

Statement + 요? / 습니까?
K-pop을 좋아해요
K-pop을 좋아해요?
Do you like K-pop?

WH- Questions

뭐/무엇 what
누구 who
언제 when
어디 where
왜 why
어떻게 how
이름이 뭐예요?
What is your name?
어디에서 왔어요?
Where are you from?
